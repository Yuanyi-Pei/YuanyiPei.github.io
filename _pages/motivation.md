## Motivation

My commitment to research is shaped by both intellectual curiosity and a sense of responsibility toward public health challenges.

What draws me most strongly to neuroscience is the opportunity to uncover hidden connections between systems that appear unrelated. I am particularly fascinated by how sleep interacts with memory, motor control, and neurodegenerative vulnerability. The process of designing experiments, refining surgical approaches, and identifying mechanistic links between seemingly independent phenomena is deeply rewarding to me. Research, for me, is a disciplined form of curiosity — a structured way to ask difficult questions and pursue rigorous answers.

At the same time, my scientific interests are grounded in real-world concerns. Participating in the NYC Walk to End Alzheimer’s reinforced my awareness of the human dimension behind neurodegenerative disease. I am motivated by the belief that careful, mechanistic research — even when focused on fundamental circuits — can ultimately contribute to long-term improvements in patient care and quality of life.

I hope to build a research career that integrates curiosity-driven discovery with meaningful social impact. For me, science is not only an intellectual pursuit, but also a sustained commitment to understanding and alleviating human suffering through knowledge.

My time at the Chinese Institute for Brain Research (CIBR) profoundly shaped my understanding of what it means to “enjoy science.” 

Under the mentorship of Dr. Akihiro Yamanaka, I began to appreciate that enjoying science does not simply mean celebrating results. It means embracing uncertainty, designing careful experiments, refining surgical techniques repeatedly, and remaining patient when answers do not come immediately. It means finding satisfaction in the process of thinking — in connecting observations that initially seem unrelated and allowing questions to evolve through disciplined inquiry.

What I value most from that period is the research atmosphere itself: long discussions at the bench, collaborative problem-solving, and the quiet persistence required in experimental neuroscience. That environment helped me realize that scientific joy comes from curiosity sustained by rigor. It is a mindset I hope to carry forward throughout my research career.
