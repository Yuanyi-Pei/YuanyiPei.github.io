I am an MS candidate in Biomedical Sciences at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai. I received my BSc in Biological Sciences from China Agricultural University.

My research focuses on sleep neurobiology and hypothalamic circuits, particularly the role of orexinergic neurons in regulating arousal, motor control, and sleep homeostasis. I am interested in understanding how sleep-dependent neural mechanisms shape behavior and how dysfunction in these systems contributes to neurodegenerative disorders such as Alzheimer’s disease.

At the Chinese Institute for Brain Research (CIBR), I worked on optimizing compact human prepro-orexin promoters for AAV-mediated targeting of orexin neurons, aiming to improve viral packaging efficiency while maintaining high specificity and coverage. I have also investigated the neural circuits linking sleep pressure with motor nuclei controlling eyelid closure using retrograde tracing and immunohistochemistry.

Currently, I am preparing for thesis research involving in vivo electrophysiology and behavioral analysis in sleep models. My long-term goal is to uncover systems-level mechanisms underlying sleep regulation and circuit dysfunction.


## Research Interests

My research aims to understand how sleep homeostasis reorganizes neural circuits to support memory consolidation and long-term brain health.

I am particularly interested in:

- Neural mechanisms underlying sleep regulation and slow-wave activity  
- Hypothalamic orexin/hypocretin circuits in arousal and state transitions  
- Sleep-dependent memory consolidation and hippocampal–cortical plasticity  
- Functional interactions between sleep circuits and motor systems  
- Mechanisms linking sleep disruption to Alzheimer’s disease pathology

## Independent Research

### Optimization of Compact Human Prepro-Orexin Promoters for Selective Targeting of Orexin Neurons
This project aimed to improve the specificity and efficiency of viral targeting of orexin/hypocretin neurons in wild-type mice. 

The widely used 3.2 kb human prepro-orexin promoter approaches the packaging limit of AAV vectors, restricting the incorporation of additional functional elements. To address this limitation, I designed and engineered truncated promoter variants by strategically removing Alu repeat sequences while preserving critical regulatory elements (OE1 and OE2).

Using systemic AAV2/PHP.eB delivery, I evaluated promoter performance through quantitative immunohistochemical analysis of coverage and specificity within the lateral hypothalamus. 

The optimized 2.2 kb and 0.4 kb variants achieved substantially improved neuronal coverage while maintaining high specificity, thereby expanding the effective cargo capacity for circuit-level manipulation in sleep research.

This work is currently being prepared for manuscript submission.

My undergraduate thesis explored the neural mechanisms underlying eyelid closure during sleep, motivated by a fundamental question: how does sleep pressure influence motor output systems?

Using retrograde tracing from the orbicularis oculi muscle, I mapped the distribution of facial motor neurons (7N) controlling eyelid closure and characterized their neurotransmitter identity through immunohistochemistry. I established a high-precision facial muscle injection protocol in mice and identified a clustered population of cholinergic neurons located in the lateral posterior region of the facial nucleus.

This project originated from my curiosity about the relationship between sleep homeostasis and motor control, particularly in the context of nocturnal lagophthalmos and sleep-related disorders. Although technically challenging, the process of refining surgical methods and validating circuit-level specificity strengthened my commitment to systems neuroscience research.

Through this work, I developed a deeper interest in how sleep-dependent neural dynamics interact with motor and cognitive systems — a question that continues to shape my current research direction.
